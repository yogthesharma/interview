# Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical decision. What did you do?

**Target time:** 60–90 seconds (STAR format)

---

## Talk track (TypeScript migration at IQM — strong fit from resume)

> **Situation:** At IQM we had a growing **JavaScript** codebase across 10+ internal workflows. Runtime errors in prod were costing us time, and new devs struggled without types.
>
> **Disagreement:** Some folks wanted to **stay on JavaScript** and add gradual JSDoc — faster short term, less churn. I believed we needed a proper **TypeScript migration** with stricter typing on API boundaries and shared models.
>
> **What I did:** I didn't push in meetings with ego. I:
> - Built a **small pilot** — migrated one module, measured fewer prod bugs and better IDE support
> - Shared concrete before/after — typed API contracts with backend, fewer `undefined` crashes
> - Proposed a **phased rollout** — not big-bang — so the team felt less risk
>
> **Result:** We moved forward with the migration. I helped drive it across modules. Runtime errors dropped and onboarding new devs got easier.
>
> **Learning:** Disagree with **data and a path**, not opinions. And I wasn't a lead — I influenced through **proof and helping execute**, not authority.

---

## Bullet spine

1. JS vs TS — real disagreement
2. Pilot + metrics — not just "I was right"
3. Phased approach — met skeptics halfway
4. Outcome — fewer errors, better DX
5. IC influence, not title
